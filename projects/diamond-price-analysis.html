Overview

This project conducts a comprehensive statistical analysis of diamond pricing factors, with a specific focus on round-shaped diamonds in the 0.7-0.89 carat range. By examining various attributes that influence diamond value, the analysis provides insights into what truly determines a diamond's market price in this specific weight category.

Problem Statement

Diamond pricing is complex and often opaque to consumers. While the "4Cs" (carat, cut, color, clarity) are well-known factors, their relative importance and interaction effects are not always clear, especially within specific carat ranges. This project aimed to answer: What are the most significant determinants of diamond prices specifically in the 0.7-0.89 carat range, and how can this knowledge help consumers make more informed purchasing decisions?

My Approach

I collected a dataset of over 5,000 round diamonds within the 0.7-0.89 carat range, including detailed information on their physical attributes and market prices. After cleaning and preprocessing the data, I performed exploratory data analysis to identify initial patterns and relationships.

I then conducted statistical analyses including correlation studies, multiple regression modeling, and ANOVA tests to quantify the impact of each attribute on price. I also examined interaction effects between variables to understand how combinations of features influence pricing beyond their individual contributions.

To validate the findings, I developed predictive models and tested them against a holdout sample of diamond prices, measuring accuracy through RMSE and R-squared metrics.

Tools & Technologies

Python Pandas NumPy Matplotlib Seaborn Scikit-learn Statsmodels

Key Insights

Cut Quality Premium

Within the 0.7-0.89ct range, the premium for an "Excellent" cut over a "Very Good" cut was approximately 15-20%, significantly higher than in other carat ranges. This suggests that cut quality becomes particularly important as diamonds approach the psychologically significant 1-carat threshold.

Color-Clarity Trade-off

The analysis revealed an optimal "sweet spot" where slightly sacrificing color grade (e.g., going from D to F) while maintaining a higher clarity grade often resulted in better value for consumers. This trade-off was more pronounced in this carat range than in smaller diamonds.

Price Discontinuities

Sharp price jumps were observed at specific thresholds (e.g., between VS1 and VVS2 clarity), rather than a continuous price gradient. These discontinuities create opportunities for value-conscious consumers to position their purchases just below premium thresholds.

Conclusion

The Diamond Price Analysis project successfully identified the key factors driving diamond prices in the 0.7-0.89 carat range. The findings challenge some conventional wisdom about diamond valuation and provide actionable insights for consumers looking to maximize value in this specific market segment.

Future work could expand this analysis to other diamond shapes, explore temporal price trends, or incorporate additional factors such as fluorescence and certification differences. The methodologies developed in this project could also be applied to other luxury goods markets where pricing factors are complex and multidimensional.
